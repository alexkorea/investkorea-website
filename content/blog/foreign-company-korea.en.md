<h2 id="introduction">1. Introduction</h2>
Korea is an attractive market for foreign companies. However, selecting the right type of business entity is crucial for a successful market entry. There are several methods available for foreign corporations to enter the Korean market, each with its own advantages and disadvantages. Therefore, it is essential to clearly understand and compare the characteristics of subsidiaries, branch offices, and liaison offices.

<h2 id="types">2. Types of Market Entry</h2>
Foreign companies can enter Korea through three main types: subsidiary (local corporation), branch office, and liaison office. Each type differs in legal status, tax obligations, and operational methods. Understanding these differences is the first step toward success in the Korean market.

<h2 id="subsidiary">3. Subsidiary (Local Corporation)</h2>
A subsidiary involves a foreign company establishing an independent legal entity within Korea. Such a corporation operates under Korean law and has the authority to conduct business directly in the Korean market. Establishing a subsidiary requires a minimum capital investment and is subject to corporate taxes and other levies.

<h2 id="branch">4. Branch Office</h2>
A branch office is a form that allows the parent company to directly carry out its business activities in Korea. A branch is not classified as a separate legal entity; it operates as an extension of the parent company. A branch does not require separate corporate registration and follows the parent company's accounting system. However, a branch must pay taxes on income generated in Korea.

<h2 id="liaison">5. Liaison Office</h2>
A liaison office is aimed at conducting preliminary research and gathering information for the foreign company's entry into the Korean market. This office cannot engage in direct revenue-generating activities and primarily handles tasks such as collecting data for the head office and managing customer relationships. A liaison office is relatively simple to establish and operate.

<h2 id="regulations">7. Legal Regulations</h2>
Each entity type is subject to different legal regulations. A subsidiary must register its business in accordance with the Korean Commercial Act and meet various requirements. A branch office also requires specific registration procedures, and while a liaison office faces relatively fewer regulations, certain registration procedures are still necessary.

<h2 id="benefits">8. Advantages of Each Type</h2>
Each entity type has its own unique advantages. A subsidiary can take advantage of numerous market opportunities, while a branch office benefits from the support of its parent company. A liaison office can reduce risk through market research and customer management at relatively low cost.

<h2 id="challenges">9. Challenges of Each Type</h2>
Each entity type comes with its own challenges. Subsidiaries face high establishment and operating costs, branches require coordination with the parent company, and liaison offices may miss business opportunities due to their limited operational scope. These are factors that every company must consider carefully.

<h2 id="conclusion">10. Conclusion</h2>
While there are various methods for foreign companies to enter the Korean market, it is essential to understand the pros and cons of each approach in depth. Choosing the entity type that best suits your business model and strategy is key to achieving a successful entry into the Korean market.
