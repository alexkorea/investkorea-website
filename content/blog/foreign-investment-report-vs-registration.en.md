# Practical Guide to the Difference Between Reporting and Registration under the Foreign Investment Promotion Act

When foreigners invest in Korea, the concepts of “reporting” and “registration” under the Foreign Investment Promotion Act (FIPA) are frequently confused. At their core, “reporting” refers to the pre-investment process required by law, while “registration” is the official step where the government records the completed investment. Each serves a distinct purpose, requires different documents, and is handled by different agencies.

In practice, it’s essential to complete the “foreign investment report” before proceeding with the investment, setting up a corporation, or applying for a visa. “Registration,” on the other hand, is crucial for proving the investment post-completion and for accessing preferential government programs or administrative benefits. According to Articles 5 to 8 of the FIPA, reporting involves submitting forms to the relevant authorities (banks or local governments), while registration evidences the finalized investment through certificates like the Foreign Investment Registration Certificate. Below, we break down these procedures and highlight the practical distinctions.

## <h2 id="section-1">1. Overview of the Foreign Investment Promotion Act</h2>

The Foreign Investment Promotion Act (“the Act”) was established to encourage investment in Korea by simplifying the process and providing benefits for foreign investors. Depending on the type of capital, investment method (such as equity acquisition or assets like equipment or intellectual property), or the investor's status, both reporting and registration may be required.

- **Legal basis:** FIPA, Articles 5–8
- **Responsible agencies:** KOTRA, city/county/district offices, banks handling foreign exchange

Foreign investors must submit a report before making an investment, and complete the registration process afterward in order to proceed with things like visa applications or utilize special incentives.

## <h2 id="section-2">2. Definitions & Legal Basis: Reporting vs Registration</h2>

### Reporting

- **Definition:** The procedure of submitting a report to relevant authorities before the investment, to verify legal compliance.
- **Legal basis:** FIPA Articles 5 and 6
- **Agencies involved:** City/county/district government offices where the target company is registered, KOTRA, banks handling foreign exchange
- **Purpose:** To verify investment requirements, reject ineligible investments, and provide eligibility for later incentives/support

### Registration

- **Definition:** The process of officially registering and certifying the completion of the investment (capital remittance, share acquisition, etc.) after reporting.
- **Legal basis:** FIPA Article 7
- **Agencies involved:** Banks handling foreign exchange
- **Purpose:** To enable the government to officially record the investment, providing a legal basis for incentives and preferential treatment

Reporting is required before the investment can begin, while registration confirms completion and serves as an administrative certification. Both are linked to corporation establishment, visa issuance, and access to government support measures.

## <h2 id="section-3">3. Foreign Investment Reporting Procedure & Main Documents</h2>

The reporting phase verifies the investor's eligibility under the law through a preliminary review.

**Summary of steps:**
1. Select the target company for investment
2. Prepare and complete the reporting forms and related documents
3. Submit the documents to the appropriate agency (bank/KOTRA/local government)
4. Agency reviews the documentation, issues an investment report certificate

**Main documents required:**
- Foreign Investment Report Form
- Copy of passport
- Investment contract
- Articles of incorporation and the commercial registry of the target company
- Proof of investor’s identity/status
- Other documents as requested by the agency

Once reporting is completed, the investor receives a “report certificate,” which is required to move forward with capital remittance, company establishment, and visa applications.

## <h2 id="section-4">4. Foreign Investment Registration Procedure & Required Documents</h2>

This stage verifies the tangible completion of the investment (remittance of funds, acquisition of shares/assets) following reporting.

**Summary of steps:**
1. Actual transfer of investment funds (foreign or local currency)
2. Completion of share or asset acquisition by the target company
3. File the investment completion notice and registration application with the foreign exchange bank or responsible agency
4. Receive official documentation, such as the Foreign Investment Registration Certificate

**Main documents required:**
- Investment report certificate
- Evidence of fund transfer (remittance slip, deposit receipt, etc.)
- Proof of share acquisition (shareholder registry/commercial registry extract)
- Foreign Investment Registration application form
- Additional documents as requested by the bank

Upon successful registration, the investor receives a Foreign Investment Registration Certificate, which forms the basis for applying for government support, tax benefits, visas, and other incentives.

## <h2 id="section-6">6. Common Mistakes & Precautions</h2>

In-depth experience shows that certain mistakes and requirements come up frequently in the practical handling of foreign investment.

> **Warning: Failing to complete the reporting process means you cannot remit funds, establish a company, or apply for a visa. Missing registration can result in disqualification from government incentives and visa applications. Always consult a qualified administrative specialist for guidance.**

### Typical Mistakes
- Sending investment funds or registering the company before completing the reporting process
- Applying for a business (D-8) visa without a report certificate
- Neglecting registration after remitting funds
- Differences in document requirements between agencies: not using the latest forms or checking requirements
- Not verifying investor eligibility or target company suitability
- Submitting duplicate documents to the same agency by confusing reporting and registration

### Key Precautions
- Laws and forms can change depending on the agency or time period, so verify the latest guidelines with relevant authorities (foreign exchange banks, KOTRA, local government) before investing
- Since follow-up processes like company establishment and visa issuance are tied to these procedures, careful management of documents at each stage is essential

## <h2 id="section-7">7. FAQ: Frequently Asked Questions about FIPA Reporting & Registration</h2>

### Q1: Can reporting and registration be done at the same time?
**A1:** No. Reporting must be completed before investment, and registration is done after investment is finalized. The two procedures must be followed sequentially.

### Q2: What happens if I skip the reporting process?
**A2:** If you remit investment funds or establish a corporation without reporting, you may be restricted from visa applications or government support. Reporting is a mandatory step in practice.

### Q3: Which agencies handle reporting and registration?
**A3:** You can report to KOTRA, local governments, or foreign exchange banks; registration is typically handled by foreign exchange banks. Document requirements may vary by agency.

### Q4: Is there anything special to consider when remitting funds after reporting?
**A4:** After receiving the report certificate, remit the funds via a foreign exchange bank, making clear the purpose is for investment. Confirm the guidelines on remittance and capital deposit procedures.

### Q5: Can I apply for a (D-8) business visa without a Foreign Investment Registration Certificate?
**A5:** No. A registration certificate is required for visa applications and follow-up administrative processing. Registration is essential for practical purposes.
